Project Overview

"What Will It Take?" is a speculative exploration of a US city with plublic transit replacing the roads and freeways. The audience experiences this through a narrative progression of the day-to-day life of someone who lives in both the present day city and the possible future city.

The audience is intended to be for adults who have experience driving for their needs and can relate to the story, while also having voting power. It is made to appeal to the US citizen and their inner turmoils.

Through exploring this website I hope that the audience feels more inclined towards supporting public transit and know more clearly why they would do that. They will also gain a more first-hand experience that will make them curious about how transport worsens social inequality.

Project Research

A lot of research went into this project namely because of all the numbers involved. Things like the cost of each trip didn't come out of nowhere, they are calculated from official esitmates from agencies like the EPA. When I told others about the attempts at accuracy, they seemed to enjoy that aspect so I leaned into it with the several screens that show different types of data in different ways.

At first, the project had too wide of a scope. It aimed to do too many things and didn't have a direct audience. I changed this by thinking about how I can make a linear experience rather than an open one. Seeing what my classmates were working on also inspired me because there were many story-based ones that I felt carried more engaging stories.

Visual Design

There are four main sections: the map, story, home, and data. The map is visual inspired by routing apps like Google Maps and Waze. I wanted the audience to feel like they're actually trying to go to work/store/school. The story screen uses only colors from gray to red. This is meant to incite a sense of danger and gloom. The future illustrations, however, have a lot more brighter pinks and reds that make it feel happier.

The home is mainly but not entirely purple hues. I didn't want it to be the same gloomy feeling as the story, but a neutral look at what's happening narratively. The purple creates a feeling of more detachment than intimacy. On the data screens, I used no color and only black and white. The contrast insinuates the clear comparisons of public transit versus personal motor vehicles.

User Interface

There are several types of user interface compoments that I included. It begins with a large question and a form with a submit button. It's very simple and there only being one option per selection makes it cognitively not overwhelming. The map is also organized in a way that separates information from the visual information of the routes. It isn't often that buttons or info overlap.

The map also has popovers that explain the road colors initially. This is a prelude to when it explains the trains and trams in the second half. It also carries an authority, so when the user cannot go on the route, the popovers serve as the authoritive voice.

The living room screen with the tablet is very skeumorphic in that the button of the tablet is really how the tablet screen turns on. The tablet also wiggles as if you touched it. The screen is then divided like how a Windows screen might look with grids of information. It keeps everything organized, compact, and readable. It was also an opportunity to be unique with each grid square in how the information is presented. The weather one is compact and gives information about the whole week, whereas the gas prices is just the icon and price. The contrasts makes the tablet visually interesting to look at.

Revisions

I had a few revisions, most of them happening before production began. The idea was originally that a person could input any two addresses to compare the present to the possible future. This is however outside the scope of the class and would require a lot of complex coding. I got feedback about the directions to take it in, and ultimately ended up with a narrative visual novel style experience instead of a speculative tool kind of project.

As I worked on the project, I tried to feel out the pacing and change the animations and button locations to keep it more interesting. Some advice I liked but I didn't have time to implement. These features were adding a progress bar and adding more engaging visuals to the black and white data screens. I would definitely put more time into the asset creation.

Summary

I learned mostly from the experience of coding the project and having to go over it again and again. A big challenge was the code becoming really daunting to work with because it was really messy and interconnected so it was hard to add new features on a whim. It's probably the reason there isn't a progress bar or any way to select what chapter the user is on.

I think learning to pre-plan better and organize code before writing it is something valuable I learned. I thought it was rewarding working with the map. Nothing worked at first but when it started working it felt really good. I also really think the narrative flow of the project is something I'm happy with and I feel like it elicits emotions the way I want.

The idea of the project was something I was also proud of. It felt like it was a wonderous idea when I first thought of the speculative future. I think this idea can definitely be explored more.
